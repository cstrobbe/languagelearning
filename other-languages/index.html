Rare and Endangered Languages

General Information

  • What is the definition of “rare language”?: question on Linguistics Stack Exchange.
  • Outside of Modern Hebrew, do any previously dead languages have native speakers again?: question on Linguistics Stack Exchange.
  • Tribalingual: organisation founded in 2016 that tries to find speakers of rare languages who can create a (basic) language course for their language, so other people can learn it online.
  • UNESCO: FAQ on Endangered Languages.
  • Language Revival: Securing the Future of Endangered Languages: a self-paced MOOC on edX.
  • Mercator: Teaching Endangered Languages.
  • The Endangered Language Fund is an American organisation founded in 1996 with the goal of supporting endangered language preservation and documentation projects. (See also @EndLangFund on Twitter.)
  • The Endangered Languages Project is a project by the Alliance for Linguistic Diversity that started in 2012. One of the website's features is a catalogue of endangered languages compiled by linguists of the University of Hawaiʿi and the LINGUIST List at Eastern Michigan University. (See also @_ELProject on Twitter.)
  • The Living Tongues Institute for Endangered Languages is an American non-profit research institute dedicated to documenting endangered languages around the world. (See also @livingtongues on Twitter.)
  • The Resource Network for Linguistic Diversity (RNLD) is an Australian organisation that works to advance the sustainability of Indigenous languages and to increase the participation of Indigenous peoples in all aspects of language documentation and revitalisation through training, resource sharing, networking, and advocacy. (See also @RNLDorg on Twitter.)
  • Myles: 5 Reasons to Learn an Endangered Language.
  • Roache, Rebecca: The death of languages / Should endangered languages be preserved, and at what cost?, aeon, 12.10.2017.
  • Crystal, David: Language Death. Canto Classics. Cambridge University Press, 2014. ISBN 9781107431812.
  • Nettle, Daniel; Romaine, Suzanne: Vanishing Voices: The Extinction of the World's Languages. Oxford University Press, 2002. ISBN 9780195152463.
  • Skutnabb-Kangas, Tove: Linguistic Genocide in Education or Worldwide Diversity and Human Rights?. Orient Blackswan, 2008. ISBN 978-81-250-3461-2.
  • Seyfeddinipur, Mandana: Endangered languages: why it matters, (YouTube, 17 minutes, 09.11.2015). Mandana Seyfeddinipur is director of the Endangered Languages Documentation Programme at SOAS University of London.

According to the FSI, Arabic is in group 5 (languages with significant difficulty), which require 88 weeks of intensive study (2200 hours) to reach “proficiency”, compared to 24 weeks for languages such as Spanish, French and Italian. (See Foreign Service Institute language difficulty rankings on the website Atlas & Boots.) Kaufmann learns Arabic for 60 to 90 minutes per day and assumes he will need a few years before he will be able to enjoy listening to original Arabic materials. As usual, Steve Kaufmann talks a lot about his own experiences.

Georgian

Online Resources

  • Georgian Language through Cultural Context: a website by Shorena Kurtsikidze at the University of California, Berkeley. The website contains a number of resources for learning Georgian; see for example Introductory Online Lessons in Georgian Grammar.
  • Learn Georgian online - for free using book2, a book accompanied by a Android app and an iOS app.
  • georgisch lernen / kartuli.net: German website by Ingrid Rosenburg (Berlin) with a basic grammar of Georgian, a vocabulary training module and links to other resources for learning Georgian. The site also has an associated YouTube channel: kartuli0net with a few videos that introduce the Georgian writing system.
  • United States Peace Corps in Georgia: Georgian Language: YouTube playlist containing 21 short videos (available under a Creative Commons licence).
  • Teona Kizilashvili has a number of YouTube videos with Georgian language lessons. These videos are filmed with the camera turned towards a piece of paper that Teona uses to explain various aspect of the language.
    Georgian language lesson 1 (11 minutes), Georgian language lesson 2 (12 minutes), Georgian language lesson 3 (14 minutes), Georgian language lesson 4 (13 minutes), Georgian language lesson 5 (9 minutes), Georgian language lesson 6 - Vaja Psahvela (10 minutes). Teona Kizilashvili started creating these videos in May 2017.
  • Learn Georgian with Roni is a YouTube channel by Roni Tskhoidze (Tbilisi) that started in early 2017. The videos are entirely in Georgian with subtitles English and Georgian. It has a playlist about Georgian verbs and videos such as Adjectives, Georgian Alphabet, Learn Famous Quotes in Georgian I, Vocabulary: names of [the days of the] week, To be, Grammar I, Demonstrative Pronouns, Colours. There is also a blog with a few lessons, with the first blog post Lesson 1 dating from December 2016.
  • Theophilos Porter: Introduction to My Series on the Georgian Language, Basic Georgian (20 minutes), How to Type in Georgian on Your Computer (5 minutes), The Alphabet and Sounds of Georgian (29 minutes).
    Porter admits that he is not fluent in Georgian and appears to have abandoned the series.
  • Ali Moghaddam is from Tbilisi in Georgia and has a number of YouTube videos that teach basic Georgian: Learning Georgian language with help Pr.Inesa Shalvashvili Ep1 (8 minutes), earning Georgian language with help Pr.Inesa Shalvashvili Ep2 (10 minutes), Learning Georgian language.
  • FunkyGeorgian: website that explains a few basics of Georgian and that mainly serves as a place where you can register for online Georgian language courses. The content of the courses is not described in any detail; there is no information about who created the courses.

Learning the Georgian alphabet:

  • kartuli0net: georgisches Alphabet ქართული ანბანი georgian alphabet: 4-minute video that shows how each letter is written and pronounces a few words starting with the letters being written. The example words are also listed in the description below the video.
  • Bacurius: Alphabet géorgien, a 4-minute video very similar to the above.
  • The Alphabet and Sounds of Georgian, 29-minute video that also shows how the letters are written.
  • laoshu50500: Let's learn the Georgian alphabet right now!: 12-minute video that shows the letters and their pronunciation (but not how to write them) and introduces some example words and phrases.
    The video also recommends the book Beginner's Georgian with 2 Audio CDs by Dodona Kiziria (see below).

  • Italian Language Stack Exchange: a question and answer site for students, teachers, and linguists wanting to discuss the finer points of the Italian language.
  • ItalianPod101.com is a website with an associated YouTube channel: Learn Italian with ItalianPod101.com. The YouTube channel contains many playlists covering topics such as Absolute Beginner Italian for Every Day, Italian Listening Comprehension for Absolute Beginners and Italian Pronunciation.
  • Speak Italian from Day 1 is a website with an associated YouTube channel: Italy Made Easy. The site and the channel have been created by Manu Venditti, who is a native speaker of Italian, and a linguist and translator. The website is mainly for selling a language course that uses videos, downloadable PDF documents and downloadable MP3 files to get you started in Italian, especially to help you “survive” on your first trip to Italy.
  • Learn Italian with Lucrezia is a YouTube channel by Lucrezia Oddone from Rome. The channel has dozens of videos, and playlists such as Pronunciation, Videos for Beginners, Listening Exercises and Your daily dose of Italian. Lucrezia also has a blog called Learn Italian with Lucrezia.
  • Learn Italian with Me is a YouTube channel by Luana, a native speaker of Italian. The channel has many videos, with playlists such as Learn Italian Basic, Learn Italian Intermediate, Learn Italian Advanced and Italian lifestyle.
  • OneWorldItaliano is a YouTube channel by The One World Language Centre, an Italian language school in Cagliari (Sardinia). The channel has a large number of videos in playlists such as Corso Elementare (A1), Corso Pre-Intermedio (A2), Corso Intermedio (B1) and Corso di Italiano - Lezioni di Italiano con Veronica (more than 80 videos).
    The associated website One World Italiano offers online Italian language courses. (For the schools's face-to-face courses, see One World - Scuola di Italiano a Cagliari, Sardegna.
  • Weilà Tom is a YouTube channel with a playlist of over 40 “Learn Italian” videos. (The other videos on the channel are mostly about learning English for native speakers of Italian.)
  • ILUSS r.p.: Free Italian Language Learning Resources (access to some of the resources requires registration).
  • Benny Lewis: Benny’s Top Resources for Learning Italian.
  • Best Italian Language Learning Resources, The Mezzofanti Guild (no date, possibly March 2016).
  • Online Toolkit for Medieval Italian and Latin, On the Trail of the Bianchi of 1399, 18.09.2015. (Last accessed 16.12.2019.)

Portuguese

  • Portuguese Language Stack Exchange: a question and answer site for linguists, teachers and learners wanting to discuss the finer points of the Portuguese language.
  • Steely, Lauren: Hacking Portuguese. The creator of the website isfrom Carlifornia and started learning Portuguese in 2010.
  • Das Erste Portugiesische Lesebuch für Anfänger. Stufen A1 und A2: textbook by Audiolego. The book contains a number of Portuguese texts with a German translation next to it. Before each text, there is a vocabulary list (so this is not a graded reader).
    The English version of the book is First Portuguese Reader for Beginners by Paula Tavares (ISBN 978 1469960458).
    First Portuguese Reader for Beginners, Volume 2 by Sofia Ferreira (ISBN 978 1518817335) is a follow-up volume.

Sanskrit

  • LearnSanskrit.org.
  • Thomson, Karen; Slocum, Jonathan: Ancient Sanskrit Online, The University of Texas at Austin, Linguistics Research Center.
  • Stenzler, Adolf Friedrich: Elementarbuch der Sanskrit-Sprache (PDF), neunte Auflage, umgearbeitet von Karl F. Geldner. Gießen: Verlag von Alfred Töpelmann, 1915. This book provides a short introduction (120 pages) to classical Sanskrit (not Vedic Sanskrit). The file is a scanned version of the printed book without OCR, so searching or copying from the text is not possible.
  • Maurer, Walter; Fields, Gregory P. (editor): The Sanskrit Language: An Introductory Grammar and Reader Revised Edition. Routledge, 2002. (888 pages) ISBN 9780415491433.
  • The Lehrstuhl für Indologie at the Julius-Maximilians-Universität Würzburg recommends the following books:
    • Coulson, Michael: Sanskrit: an introduction to the classical language. London, 2006.
    • Guhe, Eberhard: Einführung in das klassische Sanskrit. Ein Lehrbuch mit Übungen. Wiesbaden, 2008.
    • Lehmann, Thomas: Sanskrit für Anfänger. Ein Lehr- und Übungsbuch. Heidelberg, 2011.
  • Stiehl, Ulrich: Sanskrit Web.
  • Gutman, Alejandro: Sanskrit Classes Online. Alejandro Gutman lives in Argentina and offers one-to-one tuition in English or Spanish through Skype or Google Hangouts.
  • Stiehl, Ulrich: Sanskrit-Kompendium.
  • Cologne Digital Sanskrit Dictionaries.
  • Apte Sanskrit Dictionary Search.
  • The Sanskrit Heritage Site.
