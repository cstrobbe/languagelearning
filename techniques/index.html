Recommended Techniques

  • Spaced repetition.

Gold List “Method/Methodology” or Goldlist Method

The “gold list method” is a vocabulary learning technique (not a method) invented by Viktor D. Huliganov / David J. James / Uncle Davey. The technique is based on the forgetting curve that Hermann Ebbinghaus discovered in the late 19th century. Its goal is long-term memory acquisition.

The FORCE Cycle

Keith Swayne (known as “CanadianLinguist” on YouTube) formulated a technique that he dubbed the FORCE cycle. It is a strategy for building spoken proficiency in your target language. See the following blog posts and the YouTube videos they contain:

  • The FORCE Cycle – Phase 1
  • The FORCE Cycle – Phase 2
  • The FORCE Cycle – Phase 3
  • The FORCE Cycle – Phase 4
  • The FORCE Cycle – Phase 5

The FORCE cycle is a system to improve your speaking and listening skills in a foreign language. The method relies on speaking in your target language and preparing for this by anticipating certain things (vocabulary and grammar) that may come up in the conversation. The idea is that you work through a specific cycle while talking to native speakers of your target language. Each FORCE cycle should bring you closer to your long-term objectives in your target language. FORCE stands for Focus, Organise, Rehearse, Communicate, Evaluate.

The first phase, focus, is about things that you will need soon and often. These things should be part of your life and that you would also talk about in your native language, e.g. your family, your language learning, your work, your home country, your native language. There are two types of focus: a topic or theme to talk about, and a skill (e.g. how to order coffee, how to receive or accept an invitation). The focus should be specific; you should to yourself something like, “I am going to learn how to buy a hard drive for my computer in Spanish.” By limiting the focus, you make the preparation of the conversation more manageable.

The second phase, organise, is about collecting vocabulary, phrases and language patterns that you will use to talk about the topic that you selected from your focus. Memorising the vocabulary and grammar patterns is not sufficient to become bilingual, you also need to use them. You can collect the vocabulary and phrases from all kinds of sources: textbooks, phrase books, advertisements, Wikipedia articles, conversations you overhear, films, etcetera. Swayne recommends writing these things down by hand instead of using a smart phone or a computer. When you ask native speakers how do say certain things, you need to be careful about how you phrase your question. Don't ask for translations of sentences in your native languages, but ask questions like, “Tell me how you would shop for a smart phone in Spanish.” This will give much more authentic input. (A good dictionary will also provide sample phrases that you can use.) The amount of material you organise depends on the level of proficiency you have: at the beginner level, you should limit the materials to a single page; you can increase this as your proficiency increases.

The third phase, rehearse, is about rehearsing the materials you collected during the “organise” phase until you know that you can use them in conversation. You should not take on too much; the goal is to say much but to say it well. Make sure that you can pronounce the words and phrases well. You should say them out loud. Swayne recommends learning the International Phonetic Alphabet, which is used in many dictionaries. He also recommends Forvo, a site where you can find the pronunciation of words in many languages. If you have recordings, learn to imitate them as closely as you can. If you are confronted with long words or phrases, it is more efficient to start with the last syllable (or word) and gradually add the parts in front of it; this is more efficient than moving from the first syllable (or word) to the last.

The fourth phase, communicate, is about the actual conversations. Conversations are the most natural way of learning languages. Using a foreign language by communicating with people who know the language better than you will ensure steady progress. The goal of the conversation is not to dominate it but to keep it going so you get a lot of input in your target language. You should also be prepared for comments about how well (or badly) you speak the language. You need to be prepared for this, for example, by learning the culturally appropriate way to respond to compliments. (For example, in Chinese and Japanese, you are supposed to be humble or modest about your skills.) The conversations need not be too long, to avoid that you get tired or lose track. Swayne recommends 15 minutes or less at the beginning, and that you gradually build up to longer conversations later on. You will also need to get good at circumlocution: if you don't know a certain word, try to describe the concept instead of relying on translations. Sometimes you will have to guess the meaning of words or phrases based on context, body language and other clues. It is also useful to learn to say, “I don't know yet how to say that in your language.”

In the last phase, evaluate, you take notes on a few specific questions. Swayne recommends that you do this using pen and paper instead of in your head. The questions are the following:

  • Was your focus specific enough or broad enough? How could it have been better?
  • Was your speaking partner appropriate and was the location of the conversation suitable?
  • Was the conversation too long or too short? If you felt exhausted afterwards, it was too long.
  • Were the words and phrases you prepared for your cycle appropriate and sufficient?
    Are there any learning resources that would help you prepare your organisation phase much better? (For example, books with vocabulary organised by theme.)
  • Were you understood completely, partly or just a little bit? What were the difficulties (on either side)? Did you rehearse enough? Did you anticipate the responses of the other person?
  • Did you understand what was said to you? How much did you understand? Did you have to guess a lot? Was your vocabulary adequate?
  • Were you confused by unfamiliar language patterns?
  • Did you have difficulty understanding words or sounds?
  • Was your FORCE cycle suitable for your proficiency level?
  • Did you exchange real information without using your native language (or another language that you know better)?
  • Did you handle misunderstandings effectively? (Strategies include clarification, repetition, confirmation, paraphrasing and guessing. You should use these strategies before falling back to your native language or another bridge language.)
  • Could your speaking partner offer any helpful feedback on how to communicate better?
  • Can you fix your earlier mistakes in your next cycle?
  • Were you courteous, friendly and culturally appropriate in your conversation? (For example, many languages have formal and informal forms of address, and these need to be used appropriately.)
  • Did you notice any new words, phrases or language patterns that you need to master? If yes, write them down so you can add them to a future cycle. (This is why the evaluation phase needs to be done immediately after the communication phase.)
  • What would have been helpful to you that you didn't know when you went into the communication phase?
  • What should be the focus of the next cycle? (This should be something that interests you.)
  • Did you learn anything new about the target language culture?
  • Evaluate your pronunciation, especially in the first six months of your study. Ask people to comment on your pronunciation. For example, ask whether your pronunciation is difficult to understand, fairly easy to understand most of the time, not a problem, or very good. If people talk to you without pausing or adjusting your language to you, that is probably a good sign.
  • Evaluate the level of understanding in both directions. Write down your successes, not just your mistakes.

Pronunciation

  • Breede, Alex: How to Amaze Natives with Your Foreign Language Pronunciation, Fluent U Blog, 24.06.2015.
  • Baytukalov, Timur: Learning Pronunciation, EasyPronunciation.com, 2017.
  • Northbrook, Julian: English Speaking Practice | How to improve your English Speaking and Fluency: SHADOWING (Julian Northbrook on YouTube, 11 minutes, 29.08.2013). This video introduces the technique, i.e. how it works and its benefits. Julian advises using audio content that is at or just a bit above your current level. For the first demonstration, he uses a text that he has read several times to make sure that he understands it completely. For the second demonstration, he shadows the audio without using the text. You should use the same audio content for a whole week, or until you feel that you “become” the speaker in the audio. (Using a transcript of the audio is something that other language learners or teachers don't mention or recommend. Also, other people use headphones, but that does not work well for a demo.)
  • Skenderoff, Vladimir: Shadow no More, VirtuallyNATIVE, 03.01.2017. Vladimir Skenderoff comes from Bulgaria, started learning English at the age of 27 and is author of the Kindle book Virtually Native: Learn How To Learn English - by a non-native, for the non-native (August 2016). He argues that it is better to repeat the words right after the speaker in the video, so you can hear your own voice better. This requires that you pause the video (or audio) when you repeat the words. The example he uses is the scene Good times, noodle salad from the film As Good as It Gets.
  • Skenderoff, Vladimir: SHADOWING: Revisited (watch before you try ...) (virtuallyNATIVE on YouTube, 6 minutes, 30.10.2017). Vladimir found out that shadowing is a technique that is mainly recommended for interpreter trainees, i.e. for very advanced learners, and that it is mainly for listening practice. In order to improve your pronunciation and intonation, you need to hear your own voice. Vladimir also adds that, You cannot internalise native stress-timing nor speed; you will speak your target language at the same speed as your native language. However, shadowing is much better than doing nothing. If you like doing it, you can continue doing it, but repeat after the native speaker.
  • Northbrook, Julian: Which is Better: Shadowing or Repeating? (Julian Northbrook on YouTube, 5 minutes, 17.11.2017). The video responds to a message by someone who makes the same point as Vladimir Skenderoff (see above). Repeating is a slow process. Shadowing is better for building positive motor habits. That is, you know, the physical side of fluency (intonation, rhythm, speed, etcetera).
  • Northbrook, Julian: Is Shadowing a Method? (Julian Northbrook on YouTube, 3 minutes, 26.11.2017). Shadowing is not a method but an exercise or a techique that improves one aspect of your target language.
  • Lampariello, Luca: How to Sound Like a Native Speaker: 7 Incredibly Effective Tips (Luca Lampariello on YouTube, 9 minutes, 31.10.2018).
    According to Luca, language learners who achieve a native-like pronunciation share three things: interest, method and attitude. His tips are the following:
    1. You should tackle phonetics from the very beginning. People who acquire a good accent, take interest in correct pronunciation from the very beginning.
    2. You should start with intonation first. This is also how children learn speech.
    3. Find materials that you can read while listening to it and pay attention to the pronunciation.
    4. While listening to the spoken version of a printed text, use a pencil to mark where the intonation goes up or down.
    5. Practise pronuncation by reading out loud texts that you have listened to. Do not simple follow along with the audio; you need to deeply understand the meaning and the sound and how they related. (Luca demonstrates how slowly you should try to read.)
    6. Interleave your learning activities to provide variety.
    7. Get feedback. You can record your voice to listen to your pronunciation, but you can also send it to a native speaker.

Various Vocabulary Learning Techniques

  • Use a Notebook to Learn More English Vocabulary - Increase English Vocabulary (Oxford Online English, 17 minutes, 07.03.2017).
    Although this video is about English vocabulary, the tips it provides apply to other languages as well. The video recommends using a notebook that is small enough so you can carry it in your pocket. It describes four steps to recording new vocabulary:
    1. Recording the word clearly and correctly. Look up the word in a dictionary to make sure you get the spelling right.
    2. Record the meaning, for example using a translation into your own language or (better) using a definition in the target language. You don't need to copy out all the meanings listed in the dictionary. Drawing a picture can also help. It is also a good idea to add a word with the opposite meaning and a symbol indicating whether the word has a positive or a negative meaning.
    3. Recording the “form” of the word, possibly in brackets next to the world. You can use abbreviations such as the following: n (noun), c (countable), u (uncountable), c/u (countable or uncountable), adj (adjective), adv (adverb), v (verb), ID (idiom) and mwv (multi-word verb).
    4. Recording the pronunciation of the word, including stress.
    It can also be helpful to use colours, e.g. different colours for different parts of speech.
    The last part of the video explains how to review vocabulary. The first review should be done soon after the vocabulary has been recorded, e.g. ten minutes later. First read the vocabulary, then test yourself by covering the meanings. You can also do this in the opposite direction. Language Learning Strategies

Language learning strategies refers to the processes and actions that are consciously deployed by language learners to help them to learn or use a language more effectively (Wikipedia).
Strategies are commonly divided into learning strategies and communicative strategies, although there are other ways of categorizing them. Learning strategies are techniques used to improve learning, such as mnemonics or using a dictionary. Communicative strategies are strategies a learner uses to convey meaning even when she doesn't have access to the correct form, such as using pro-forms like thing, or using non-verbal means such as gestures. (Wikipedia, emphasis added.)

The references below cover mostly academic resources about strategies in general.

  • Center for Advanced Research on Language Acquisition (CARLA): Research on Language Learning Strategies and Styles and Strategies-Based Language Instruction: Selected Bibliography (compiled by Andrew D. Cohen; there are a few links to downloadable PDF files).
  • Cullerton, Ali: Effective Strategies for Learning a New Language (52 minutes, YouTube, December 2014).
  • Language Institute, University of Wisconsin-Madison: Language Learning Strategies, a course taught to language students by Dianna Murphy in 2009:
    • Part 1: explains what language learning strategies are, asks students what strategies they use (e.g. using flash cards for vocabulary, using the language outside class, practice every day, making associations), types of strategies (metacognitive, affective and cognitive) and discusses student motivations for learning specific languages.
    • Part 2: figuring out what kind of learner you are (learning style: visual, auditory, kinesthetic), what kind of learning tasks you enjoy (depending on where you are on the introverted-extraverted continuum, on the closure-oriented versus open continuum), how you deal with new information (global versus particular), how you commit new material to memory (sharpeners versus leveler), how you deal with language rules (deductive versus inductive).
    • Part 3: the relation between learning styles and learning strategies, “effective” learning strategies (outcomes of research on good learners).
    • Part 4: continues discussion of research on good learners, and discusses the matching of strategies and tasks. (The instructor also recommends the book How to Be a More Successful Language Learner by Joan Rubin and Irene Thompseon (Heinle, 1994).

Listening Skills

  • Kaufmann, Steve: My Top 7 Tips For Improving Your Listening Skills (YouTube, 9 minutes, 07.12.2017). Steve Kaufmann's top tips are the following. First, you need to listen to things that you have a chance of understanding; in the beginning, access to a transcript can be helpful. Second, in the initial stages, you should listen a lot to repetitious materials. (Repetitious here means that vocabulary and grammar are repeated a lot.) Steve listens to basic materials 50 or more times. Third, you should listen as soons as possible to interesting materials. Otherwise, after a while, you ability to take in new things will diminish (compared to familiar materials). Fourth, you should read a lot; not only things you are listening to. Reading helps you undestand when you listen. Fifth, vary the things that you listen to; variety is good for the brain. Once you are familiar with a certain theme, move on to something new. Sixth, keep listening instead of giving up when you don't understand something. Seventh, keep reading.

This video is based on videos and other information by polyglots such as Alexander Argüelles, Gabriel Wyne, Benny Lewis and Steve Kaufmann. Benny Lewis learned ten languages by communicating with native speakers (“speak from day one”). The polyglots in the video are all autodidacts. With regards to methods, he mentions or cites the following points:
    • The number one principle in language learning is to enjoy it. (Steve Kaufmann)
    • Input: anything that gets you to read or listen to your target language, including course books, Assimil courses and Linguaphone courses. When practising intensive reading, use texts that are interesting and short; do this when you have the most mental energy and for 30-35 minutes (maximum) at a time (Luca Lampariello). Extensive reading can be done for longer periods of time; content needs to be interesting and at your reading level (98%, according to Argüelles). You can start with graded readers.
    • Output: find native speakers and talk to them. You can find people online. When you should talking to native speakers is a matter of debate. Social learners may benefit from Lewis' “speak from day one” rule.
    • Spaced repetition systems: for example with Duolingo, Memrise, Anki or the Goldlist method. (You can even practise shadowing using Anki.) The Goldlist method was developed by David James; Luca Lampariello swears by it.
    • Immersion: this can be done in a country where your target language is spoken or by immersing your home environment into the target language.
