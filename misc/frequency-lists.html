Frequency Lists

Frequency lists, i.e. lists of the most frequently used words or phrases, have several purposes. Some language learners use them to decide what vocabulary to learn first. In the context of accessibility, they can used in processes for producing easy-to-read texts or in processes for evaluating the readability of texts. (Readability encompasses more than just vocabulary or word frequency.)

Frequency lists should be based on a corpus. Today, an electronic corpus of a million words is considered small. This page gives a non-exhaustive overview of frequency lists, organised by language or language family. Many important or “big” languages are missing from this overview due to the absence of frequency dictionaries or lists that are based on a sufficiently large corpus. For example, the following languages—each with 60 million or more native speakers—are missing: Hindi, Bengali, Punjabi, Javanese, Malay, Telugu, Vietnamese, Marathi, Tamil, Urdu and Italian. For example, the Italian English Frequency Dictionary - Essential Vocabulary: 2500 Most Used Words & 421 Most Common Verbs by J. L. Laide does not provide any information on whether it was based on a corpus. The Frequency Dictionary of Italian Words by Alphonse Juilland dates from 1973 and is probably out of date. The frequency lists listed on Wiktionary are based on very limited corpora (e.g. just subtitles). Lexiteria has frequency lists with the top 200 words for 40 languages; they may be able to produce longer lists on demand.

Frequency Lists for English

  • Davies, Mark; Gardner, Dee: A Frequency Dictionary of Contemporary American English: Word Sketches, Collocates and Thematic Lists. Routledge, 2010. (368 pages) ISBN 9780415490634.
    5,000 most frequently used words; based on data from a 385 million word corpus.
  • Quasthoff, Uwe; Fielder, Sabine; Hallsteinsdóttir, Erla: Frequency Dictionary English. Leipziger Universitätsverlag, 2012. (110 pages and a CD-ROM) ISBN 978-3-86583-654-0.
    Most frequent 1,000 words; the CD-ROM provides a longer list; no information about the size of the corpus upon which the frequency list is based.
  • Word frequency data: Corpus of Contemporary American English. 5,000-60,000 word lists, based on the 450 million word Corpus of Contemporary American English (COCA).
  • Audience Dialogue: Sustainable English - a vocabulary: list of the 15,000 commonest words, based on the 100 million word British National Corpus (BNC). See also Word frequencies in English.
  • subtlex-word-frequencies: 200,000 words sorted by frequency of use in spoken English. The frequency is based on SUBTLEX US, a corpus of subtitles from English-language movie.
  • Kaufman, Josh: google-10000-english: This repo contains a list of the 10,000 most common English words in order of frequency, as determined by n-gram frequency analysis of the Google's Trillion Word Corpus. This is not entirely orginal work: This repo is derived from Peter Norvig's compilation of the 1/3 million most frequent English words.

Frequency Lists for Other Germanic Languages

  • Tiberius, Carole; Schoonheim, Tanneke: A Frequency Dictionary of Dutch: Core Vocabulary for Learners. Routledge, 2014. (310 pages) ISBN 9780415523806.
    5,000 most frequently used words. Based on a 290 million word corpus which includes both written and spoken material. Also covers Belgian Dutch.
  • Tschirner, Erwin, Jones, Randall: A Frequency Dictionary of German: Core Vocabulary for Learners. Routledge, 2006. (190 pages) ISBN 9780415316330.
    4,034 most frequently used words. Based on a 4.2 million-word corpus which is evenly divided between spoken, fiction and non-fiction texts.
  • Quasthoff, Uwe; Fielder, Sabine; Hallsteinsdóttir, Erla: Frequency Dictionary German - Häufigkeitswörterbuch Deutsch. Leipziger Universitätsverlag, 2011. (115 pages and a CD-ROM) ISBN 978-3-86583-509-3.
    Most frequent 1,000 words; the CD-ROM provides a longer list; no information about the size of the corpus upon which the frequency list is based.
  • Quasthoff, Uwe; Fielder, Sabine; Hallsteinsdóttir, Erla: Frequency Dictionary Icelandic - Íslensk tiðniorðabók. Leipziger Universitätsverlag, 2012. (109 pages and a CD-ROM) ISBN 978-3-86583-656-4.
    Most frequent 1,000 words; the CD-ROM provides a longer list; no information about the size of the corpus upon which the frequency list is based.

Frequency Lists for Chinese Languages

  • Xiao, Richard; Rayson, Paul; McEnery, Tony: A Frequency Dictionary of Mandarin Chinese: Core Vocabulary for Learners. Routledge, 2009. (390 pages) ISBN 9780415455862.
    5,000 most common words and 2,000 most common simplified Chinese characters. Based on a fifty-million-word corpus composed of spoken, fiction, non-fiction and news texts in current use.
  • Dew, James Erwin: 6000 Chinese Words: A Vocabulary Frequency Handbook. Cheng & Tsui. (Out of print; see mention on the Linguist List, 31 March 2002.
  • Jun Da: Frequency statistics 频率统计.
    (Character frequencies, not word frequencies.)
  • Tsai, Chih-Hao: Frequency and Stroke Counts of Chinese Characters. 01.01.1996, updated 03.08.2005.
    Frequency of usage of the 13,060 (Traditional) Chinese characters defined in the Big5 coding scheme.

Corpora

Corpora for Semitic Languages

  • The Quranic Arabic Corpus, an annotated linguistic resource which shows the Arabic grammar, syntax and morphology for each word in the Holy Quran. The corpus data is available under the GNU General Public License (version 3) and can be downloaded if you provide your e-mail address.
  • arabiCorpus, developed at the College of Humanities at Brigham Young University and currently maintained by professor Dilworth B. Parkinson. The arabiCorpus project page describes the corpus as a free, untagged, 30-million-word corpus with a user-friendly interface.
  • Latifa Al-Sulaiti: Arabic Corpora: overview of both free and non-free corpora and their main characteristics (source, size, purpose and material).

Corpus and Concordance Tools

  • Andew Roberts: aConCorde: aConcorde is a multi-lingual concordance tool. Originally developed for native Arabic concordance, it posses basic concordance functionality, as well as English and Arabic interfaces. Written in Java, so will run on any platform that has the Java Runtime Environment installed.
  • Laurence Anthony: AntConc, a freeware corpus analysis toolkit for concordancing and text analysis.
  • WebCorp: The Web as a Corpus: WebCorp Live lets you access the Web as a corpus - a large collection of texts from which examples of real language use can be extracted. The tool was developed by the Research and Development Unit for English Studies (RDUES) at Birmingham City University.
